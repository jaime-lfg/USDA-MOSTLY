How to Read Grain Prices
Futures Price
The price agreed on today for corn delivered at a future date — March, May, July, September, or December contracts. Set on the Chicago Mercantile Exchange (CME), it's the global benchmark that every corn, soy, or grain deal in the world is referenced against.
💡 Think of it as the world's "stock price" for corn — moving every second based on weather, crop reports, export demand, and what traders think will happen next season.
Conv. Cash Price
What a farmer actually receives at the grain elevator today, for conventional (non-organic) corn delivered right now. Cash price is always quoted as Futures ± Basis — so the futures market is literally the foundation of every physical sale.
💡 Two elevators 50 miles apart can have different cash prices because their basis is different — driven by local supply, transportation costs, and buyer competition.
Basis
Cash minus Futures. Almost always negative — it's the local market's discount to the CME benchmark. Basis absorbs all the local factors that the global futures market can't see: storage space, trucking distance to the river, whether the local ethanol plant is running hard.
💡 Basis "strengthens" when it gets less negative (good for sellers). It "weakens" when it gets more negative (bad for sellers).
Organic Premium
Organic Cash minus Conv. Cash. The extra price organic farmers earn for certified grain. Typically $3–$8/bu for corn, $7–$12/bu for soy — but it compresses when organic acreage expands or food companies cut organic sourcing.
💡 A shrinking premium is an early signal of either growing organic supply or weakening demand from manufacturers — often visible here before it shows up in news.
⚡ Why Futures Are the Standard for Every Cash Price

No matter where corn is grown or sold — Iowa, Illinois, Germany, Brazil — the price negotiation always starts with the CME futures quote. An Illinois elevator offering "$4.40 for corn" is really saying "December futures ($4.60) minus 20 cents basis." The futures price is the anchor; basis is the adjustment.

This matters because it means a single market in Chicago sets the floor for prices paid to millions of farmers worldwide. When a drought in the Corn Belt pushes futures up $0.50, every elevator in the country raises its cash bids by roughly that same amount — automatically, within minutes.

Futures also enable hedging — the practice of locking in a price before harvest to remove uncertainty. A farmer who planted corn in April and expects to sell in November can sell December corn futures today. If prices fall by harvest, the futures gain offsets the lower cash price. If prices rise, they miss some upside — but they guaranteed their operating margin when they planted. Food companies do the same in reverse, buying futures to lock in input costs.

📍 Basis in the Real World — Three Scenarios
Strong Basis (River Elevator)
$4.55 cash − $4.60 futures
= −$0.05/bu
Barge demand is high, the elevator is competing hard for grain. Nearly no discount — a great time to sell physical corn.
Typical Inland Elevator
$4.25 cash − $4.60 futures
= −$0.35/bu
Normal range for an elevator 150 miles from the river. Covers trucking, handling, and elevator margin. What you'd see on this chart most of the year.
Weak Basis (Post-Harvest Glut)
$3.85 cash − $4.60 futures
= −$0.75/bu
Bins are full, elevators aren't competing for grain. Big discount — farmers who stored their corn and waited for basis to recover are now in the money.
Organic Basis Context
$10.20 org cash − $4.60 futures
= +$5.60/bu
Organic corn trades at a massive positive basis — the organic premium overwhelms the typical negative basis. The premium reflects certification costs, identity preservation, and constrained supply.
🌿 What Moves Organic Cash Prices?

Organic cash doesn't trade on an exchange — there's no "organic CME." Instead it floats on top of conventional futures as a negotiated premium, pulled in different directions by five forces:

① Conventional Futures
The floor. No organic seller will take less than what conventional pays. When CME corn rallies, organic follows — but with a lag since it's not traded in real time.
② Certified Organic Supply
Less than 1% of US corn acres are certified organic. A bad season hits this tiny pool hard — one drought year can spike the premium significantly. New certified acres take 3 years to come online.
③ Buyer Demand
Organic dairies, poultry farms, and food manufacturers are the main buyers. When consumer demand for organic products softens — like during inflation squeezes — these buyers pull back and the premium compresses.
④ Import Competition
Historically a large share of US organic grain was imported from Ukraine, Turkey, and Romania. When import flows are healthy, they cap the domestic premium. Disruptions cause domestic organic to spike.
⑤ Certification Costs
Organic certification, transition costs, lower yields, and segregated handling all add real costs for the farmer. The premium must stay high enough to compensate — otherwise acres don't transition.
💡 The chart tells the story: a rising organic premium means organic supply is tightening or buyer demand is picking up. A falling premium means more certified supply coming online, softer organic demand, or heavy import competition — even if the underlying futures price is flat.
Futures ± basis → Conv. Cash + organic premium → Organic Cash
